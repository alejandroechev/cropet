1. What is Evapotranspiration?

Evapotranspiration (ET) is the combined process by which water moves from the Earth's surface to the atmosphere. It has two components:

  • Evaporation ‚Äî water turning to vapor from soil, open water, and wet plant surfaces.
  • Transpiration ‚Äî water drawn up through plant roots, transported through stems, and released through leaf stomata.

ET is the largest consumer of water in agriculture, often accounting for 60‚Äì90% of all water applied to fields. Accurate ET estimation is critical for:

  • Irrigation scheduling ‚Äî applying the right amount of water at the right time
  • Water resource planning ‚Äî allocating river and reservoir water among competing uses
  • Drought monitoring ‚Äî tracking water stress across regions
  • Climate studies ‚Äî understanding water and energy balance in the atmosphere

2. Reference Evapotranspiration (ETo)

Rather than estimating ET for every crop in every condition, the FAO standardized a reference crop: a hypothetical grass surface 12 cm tall, actively growing, completely shading the ground, and well-watered.

The rate of ET from this reference surface is called ETo (reference evapotranspiration). It depends only on weather ‚Äî not on the specific crop being grown.

Why standardize? Because ETo provides a consistent baseline. To estimate water need for any specific crop, you multiply ETo by a crop coefficient (Kc):

ETc = Kc √ó ETo

This two-step approach separates weather effects from crop effects, making the method universally applicable.

4. Solar Radiation

Radiation is the primary energy source driving evapotranspiration. CropET estimates it step by step from your site's latitude and sunshine data:

Extraterrestrial Radiation (Ra)

The solar energy reaching the top of the atmosphere, computed from latitude and day of year. It varies with season and location ‚Äî more at the equator, more in summer.

Solar Radiation (Rs)

The fraction of Ra that reaches the ground, estimated from sunshine hours:

Rs = (a + b √ó n/N) √ó Ra

where n = actual sunshine hours, N = maximum possible sunshine hours, and a, b are regression constants (typically 0.25 and 0.50).

Net Radiation (Rn)

The balance of incoming and outgoing energy at the crop surface:

  • Net shortwave: Rns = (1 ‚àí Œ±) √ó Rs where Œ± = 0.23 (grass albedo)
  • Net longwave: Rnl ‚Äî thermal radiation emitted by the earth, reduced by cloud cover and humidity
  • Net radiation: Rn = Rns ‚àí Rnl

7. Crop Coefficients (Kc) Coming Soon

ETo tells you how much water a reference grass surface would use. To estimate the actual water need of a specific crop, multiply by the crop coefficient:

ETc = Kc √ó ETo

Kc varies by crop type and growth stage:

  • Kc ini (initial) ‚Äî seedling / bare soil period, typically 0.3‚Äì0.5
  • Kc mid (mid-season) ‚Äî full canopy cover, typically 0.9‚Äì1.2
  • Kc end (late season) ‚Äî senescence / harvest, typically 0.3‚Äì0.9

FAO-56 provides Kc values for over 100 crops. Crop coefficient support will be available in a future version of CropET.
